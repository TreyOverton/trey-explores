# On Authority and Authorship

**Type:** Essay material — captured from conversation, April 2026

## The Setup

A friend who works on LLM development at Google — someone smart, whose opinion carries weight — said when told about practical AI books in development: *"I don't think you should write a book about something you don't know."*

That comment sat unresolved for two months and created a stall.

## The Resolution

Working through it produced several realizations:

**His implicit model of the book** was a technical or authoritative text on AI systems — architecture, training, capabilities, safety. For that book, his criticism would be valid. That's not the book being written.

**The book being written** is about *using* AI — specifically the emergent interaction patterns that produce results, developed through systematic personal practice. That's a knowledge domain where his credential is actually weaker. He builds the engine. He doesn't spend time figuring out how a non-technical person learns to drive it effectively. Different skills, different bodies of knowledge.

**"Just send me your prompts"** revealed the gap. He sees the prompt as the unit of value because that's the input to the system he thinks about professionally. The insight he's missing: the prompt isn't the unit. The *session* is. The iterative loop is. The meta-awareness of your own priors is. He doesn't know that yet — which means he's not the audience anyway.

**The mercenary reframe:** the actual audience are people who see the book, decide the author knows more than they do, and pay accordingly. "I want to be paid well for expertise I've genuinely developed" is honest, aligns incentives correctly, and produces the same book as any nobler motivation.

## What the Stall Revealed

The critic had been granted more authority over the project than he'd earned. Once the scope of his actual expertise was correctly identified — he knows *his* domain, the author knows a different but adjacent one — the authority was correctly scoped and the stall ended.

## The Meta-Demonstration

This resolution was reached through an AI conversation. A two-month unresolved block processed to conclusion in a single exchange. That's the use case the books are trying to describe — AI as thinking partner, not output generator. The conversation that resolved the block about writing the book about the conversation style that resolved the block.
