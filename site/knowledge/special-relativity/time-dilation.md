# Time Dilation

**Status:** Understood

Moving clocks run slow. An observer watching a clock in motion relative to them will measure that clock ticking more slowly than their own. This is not an illusion or mechanical effect — it is a geometric fact about [spacetime](spacetime.md).

## The Four-Velocity Insight

This is the key to geometric understanding: every object moves through [spacetime](spacetime.md) at exactly the [speed of light](speed-of-light.md) (*c*) at all times. This is the four-velocity. The total "speed budget" is always *c*, but it's split between spatial motion and temporal motion.

- An object at rest in your frame is moving entirely through time — its full *c* budget goes to temporal motion. Its clock ticks at the maximum rate.
- An object moving through space has some of its *c* budget allocated to spatial motion, leaving less for temporal motion. Its clock ticks slower.
- A photon moves at *c* through space, allocating its entire budget to spatial motion. It has *zero* temporal motion — from the photon's perspective (to the extent that's meaningful), no time passes.

The four-velocity vector always has magnitude *c*. Spatial motion *rotates* this vector away from the time axis. This is the strongest geometric support for the [provisional belief that all observers are fundamentally fixed](../../provisional-beliefs.md) — you don't "speed up" through space, you *rotate* through spacetime.

## The Twin Paradox

Two twins start together. One travels at high speed and returns. They've aged differently — the traveling twin is younger. This is real, not a paradox.

The reframe: the twins accumulated different *spacetime path lengths*. They aren't at the same location disagreeing about time — they're at genuinely different 4D locations whose paths diverged and reconverged. The asymmetry is real because their worldlines genuinely differ (one accelerated, one didn't).
