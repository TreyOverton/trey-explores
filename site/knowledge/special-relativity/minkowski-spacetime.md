# Minkowski Spacetime

Minkowski spacetime is the specific geometric structure of [spacetime](spacetime.md) in [Special Relativity](sr-overview.md) — a flat, four-dimensional space where the geometry is defined by the Minkowski metric. It was formalized by Hermann Minkowski, Einstein's former mathematics professor, in 1907.

## What Makes It "Minkowski"

Ordinary Euclidean geometry measures distance with the Pythagorean theorem: d² = x² + y² + z². Minkowski geometry uses a different rule: the spacetime interval s² = −(ct)² + x² + y² + z². That minus sign in front of time is everything — it means time and space contribute to distances *differently*, and it's what makes the [speed of light](speed-of-light.md) an invariant boundary rather than just another velocity.

## Why "Flat"

Minkowski spacetime has no curvature — it's the spacetime equivalent of a flat plane. This is where SR operates. When gravity enters the picture, spacetime curves, and you need Riemannian geometry and General Relativity. The distinction between flat and curved spacetime is exactly [why SR is "special"](why-sr-is-special.md).

## The Human Story

Minkowski was Einstein's mathematics professor at ETH Zürich. He reportedly considered the young Einstein a "lazy dog" as a student. After Einstein published Special Relativity in 1905, Minkowski recast the theory in geometric terms that Einstein initially dismissed as "superfluous learnedness." Einstein later recognized this geometric reformulation as essential — it became the foundation for General Relativity. Minkowski died in 1909 at age 44, before seeing where his geometric insight would lead.
