# Euclidean Axioms

**Status:** Understood

Euclid's five postulates define the geometry we learn in school — flat-plane geometry where parallel lines never meet, angles of a triangle sum to 180°, and the Pythagorean theorem holds.

## The Five Postulates

1. **A straight line can be drawn between any two points**
2. **A straight line can be extended indefinitely**
3. **A circle can be drawn with any center and radius**
4. **All right angles are equal**
5. **The Parallel Postulate** — given a line and a point not on it, exactly one line through that point is parallel to the given line

## Why the Fifth Postulate Is Different

The first four postulates are simple, self-evident, and concise. The fifth is complicated, feels like it should be provable from the others, and generated 2,000 years of controversy. Mathematicians repeatedly tried to prove it as a theorem rather than accepting it as an axiom.

They failed — because it's genuinely independent. You can replace it with alternatives and get consistent, valid geometries. This realization led to [non-Euclidean geometries](non-euclidean-geometries.md).

## What About the First Four?

Even Axiom 1 breaks in certain settings. On a sphere, two antipodal points (like the North and South poles) have *infinitely many* "straight lines" (great circles) connecting them. This observation opens the door to [projective geometry](../../exploration-queue.md), finite geometries, and topology — geometries that violate even the "obvious" axioms.
