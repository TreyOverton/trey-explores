# Non-Euclidean Geometries

**Status:** Understood

Replace or remove the [parallel postulate](euclidean-axioms.md) and you get geometries that are internally consistent but describe different kinds of space — curved surfaces where straight lines behave unexpectedly.

## The Three Cases

| Geometry | Parallel postulate replacement | Curvature | Triangle angles |
|----------|-------------------------------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Euclidean** | Exactly one parallel line | Zero (flat) | Sum to 180° |
| **Hyperbolic** | Infinitely many parallel lines | Negative (saddle) | Sum to < 180° |
| **Elliptic/Spherical** | No parallel lines | Positive (sphere) | Sum to > 180° |

## Hyperbolic Geometry

Through a point not on a given line, there are *infinitely many* lines that don't intersect the given line. Space curves like a saddle or a Pringle chip. Triangles have angle sums less than 180° — the larger the triangle, the further below 180°.

## Elliptic/Spherical Geometry

There are *no* parallel lines — all "straight lines" (great circles) eventually intersect. This is the geometry of the surface of a sphere. Airlines fly great-circle routes because they're the shortest paths — these are the "straight lines" of spherical geometry.

## Why This Matters for Physics

The realization that consistent geometries with different curvature properties exist was the mathematical foundation that enabled General Relativity. If spacetime can be curved, you need a geometry that handles curvature — and these non-Euclidean geometries, generalized by Riemann, provide exactly that.

The path: [Euclidean axioms](euclidean-axioms.md) → non-Euclidean geometries → Riemannian geometry → curved spacetime → GR.
